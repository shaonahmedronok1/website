Background

Who I Actually Am

I am self-taught across every discipline that matters to me. Access to information became technically unlimited at precisely the moment that social and political pressure to ignore most of it intensified. This is an interesting place to develop an interest in information as a subject — not as a convenience, but as a force with specific architecture and specific consequences.

I run NixOS and Arch Linux not for aesthetic reasons but because I want to understand what my systems are actually doing — every process visible, every dependency explicit, nothing hidden behind a reassuring interface. This turned out to be a disposition that generalised: check the mechanism, not the label. Remove the assumption before building on it.

The reading came from that same place: Floridi on information as a physical phenomenon, Sara Walker on life as causal structure rather than chemistry, Deutsch on explanatory knowledge as the only kind that compounds. These are not casual reads. They represent an attempt to build a coherent picture of what information is, what it does to systems that process it, and why the architecture of how it flows matters more than its nominal content.

The writing followed from the reading — five essays as an attempt to think clearly in public, to find out what I actually believe when forced to defend it. The photography is a related practice: attention to what disappears without being recorded. Both are disciplines in the same thing: noticing before deciding how to classify what you've noticed.

What I want to study formally sits at the intersection of these threads — information systems, their ethical architecture, and their social consequences. I want to work in an institution where this is a research problem with methods behind it. I am seeking that opportunity.

The standard biochemical answer to "what is life?" dissolves under pressure. Walker's framework offers something sharper: life as causal structure, not chemistry. If information can determine its own future instantiation, the implications are radical.
